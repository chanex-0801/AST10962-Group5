Hong Kong Iland

Fox Spirit Legends

In the 1980s, Windsor House (originally Duke of Windsor Social Services Building) in Causeway Bay became famous for Hong Kong's most notorious fox spirit legend. A couple celebrated their baby's one-month birthday at a top-floor banquet but allegedly forgot to toast the fox spirit. That night, the mother dreamed of a red-eyed fox threatening the child; the baby was found dead the next day with all blood drained. Soon after, seven eerie fox faces appeared on the building's marble walls, drawing crowds and media frenzy. A mysterious rooftop playground was built but never opened to the public—rumored to be for the fox spirit and dead infants. The management eventually removed the wall section, but the legend persists as a warning against disrespecting spirits.


High Street Haunted House

Reports indicate that people have witnessed ghosts, strange lights, and sounds coming from abandoned corridors. Stories of the troubled souls of patients and war victims. Its eerie appearance and long period of abandonment have fueled the legend among local teenagers and the media.


Tsat Tsz Mui Road

Seven Sisters Road in North Point, Hong Kong, is named after a tragic local legend about seven young women who were as close as sisters. When the third sister was forced into an arranged marriage against their vow of lifelong celibacy, the seven sisters held hands and drowned themselves in the sea the night before the wedding. Villagers later found seven rocks of increasing size along the shore, believed to be their transformed bodies, and named them the Seven Sisters Rocks. The area became known as a dangerous swimming spot where male swimmers mysteriously drowned, fueling rumors of vengeful spirits targeting men.
