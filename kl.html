Kowloon

The hidden 3rd Track of Choi Hung Station

In the 1980s, there was an accident when Choi Hung Station was first opened to traffic. It takes only a four-minute journey on weekdays, but the staff at the next stop belatedly see the train arrive at the station, and the train loses contact after it departs. Between stations, the system failed, and only a creepy rustling sound was heard. The train then finally arrived at the station, but the conductor turned pale and refused to say what happened. Everyone in the car was frightened and sent to the hospital, and then there were rumours that a staff member had died mysteriously. After that, rumours began to spread that the tracks led to the gate of hell, when some people even said that the MTR had hired Taoist priests to the Choi Hung Station to investigate, and finally, to appease people's hearts, the MTR decided to open one more track. Since then, the MTR has not used that track. The MTR has explained that the allegedly haunted middle platform tracks actually lead to the Kowloon Bay depot.


Yau Ma Tei Station Urban Legend

Yau Ma Tei Station is linked to a famous Hong Kong urban legend about a young woman who allegedly jumped onto the tracks and then disappeared without a trace. Many people said they saw the incident, but no body or blood was found, which made the story even more mysterious. Because of this some believed it was a supernatural event, while others later argued that the woman may have survived by hiding between the tracks and leaving after the train stopped.


Ping Shek Estate

Ping Shek Estate in Kwun Tong, Hong Kong is an old public‑housing estate that has become linked with several ghost stories and urban legends. One of the most famous tales focuses on a #‑shaped block whose central courtyard forms a deep “well.” Residents and visitors say the layout feels oppressive and gloomy, and some believe that the space resembles a vertical shaft. According to the legend, ghostly suicide spirits who jump from the balconies think they can fly straight up to heaven through this well, but when they fail, they return to the ground looking for “replacements” among the living. Over time, the story has been tied to real‑life jump‑from‑balcony incidents at the estate, blurring the boundary between rumor and tragedy.


Golden Valley Theatre

The Chicken Man legend tells of human traffickers in 1930s Hong Kong who kidnapped children and tortured them to look like chicken-human hybrids for freak shows behind Gou Sing Theatre. The children had limbs amputated, tongues cut out, and feathers inserted into festering wounds before being displayed to crowds. Many died from the cruelty, and survivors haunted the area as vengeful spirits. Later revealed as fiction from a 1991 horror short story by author Sham Yuek, it became one of Hong Kong's most infamous urban legends despite being entirely made up.
